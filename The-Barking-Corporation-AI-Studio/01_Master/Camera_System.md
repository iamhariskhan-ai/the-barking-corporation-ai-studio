# Camera System

Version: 2.0

Project: The Barking Corporation

Status: Production Ready

---

# Purpose

This document defines the cinematic language used throughout The Barking Corporation.

Every shot should support storytelling, emphasize character emotion, and maintain a polished, consistent visual identity.

The camera is an invisible storyteller. It should guide the audience without distracting from the scene.

---

# Camera Philosophy

The camera should be:

• Intentional
• Stable
• Cinematic
• Character-focused
• Story-driven
• Smooth
• Natural

Avoid flashy or unnecessary movements.

The audience should notice the story—not the camera.

---

# Video Format

Aspect Ratio: 9:16 (Vertical)

Resolution: 1080 × 1920 minimum

Frame Rate: 24–30 FPS

Target Runtime: 30–45 seconds

---

# Camera Height

Default eye-level framing.

Character conversations should generally be shot from eye level.

Only change camera height to emphasize power, vulnerability, or humor.

---

# Camera Distance

## Extreme Wide Shot (EWS)

Purpose

Introduce location.

Show office environment.

Use for:

• Episode openings
• Office establishing shots

Duration

2–3 seconds

---

## Wide Shot (WS)

Purpose

Show full body.

Establish character position.

Use for:

• Walking
• Entering office
• Group scenes

Duration

2–5 seconds

---

## Medium Wide Shot (MWS)

Purpose

Show interaction between characters.

Preferred for office conversations.

---

## Medium Shot (MS)

Purpose

Default dialogue shot.

Shows upper body.

Best for:

Meetings

Office discussions

Coffee conversations

Recommended Usage

40% of all scenes.

---

## Medium Close-Up (MCU)

Purpose

Highlight emotion.

Shows:

Head

Shoulders

Hands

Ideal for reactions.

---

## Close-Up (CU)

Purpose

Focus on facial expression.

Use during:

Surprise

Embarrassment

Realization

Punchline

Duration

1–3 seconds

Avoid overuse.

---

## Extreme Close-Up (ECU)

Purpose

Highlight small details.

Examples

Eyes

Coffee mug

Slack notification

Keyboard key

Bug alert

Use sparingly.

---

# Camera Angles

## Eye Level

Default angle.

Used in 90% of scenes.

Creates equality between viewer and character.

---

## High Angle

Use when character feels:

Nervous

Embarrassed

Overwhelmed

---

## Low Angle

Use when character feels:

Confident

Powerful

Heroic

Example

Felix giving an announcement.

---

## Over-the-Shoulder (OTS)

Primary dialogue shot.

Shows:

Speaker

Listener

Context

Use frequently.

---

## Two Shot

Shows two characters equally.

Perfect for:

Buddy & Ben

Buddy & Luna

Ruby & Frank

---

## Group Shot

Three or more characters.

Keep composition clean.

Avoid overcrowding.

---

# Camera Movements

## Static

Default choice.

Use when dialogue is important.

---

## Slow Push-In

Purpose

Increase emotional intensity.

Use during:

Big realization

Important announcement

Punchline setup

---

## Slow Pull-Out

Purpose

Reveal situation.

End conversations.

Transition to next scene.

---

## Tracking Shot

Camera follows character.

Use for:

Walking

Hallway scenes

Office tour

Movement should be smooth.

---

## Pan

Reveal new information.

Follow character movement.

Keep movement slow.

---

## Tilt

Reveal:

Tall objects

Characters standing

Whiteboards

Avoid excessive tilting.

---

## Orbit Shot

Rarely used.

Suitable only for:

Celebrations

Major reveals

Never use for normal dialogue.

---

# Comedy Timing

Camera should help deliver jokes.

Examples

Pause before punchline.

Reaction close-up after joke.

Hold awkward silence.

Slow push-in for dramatic irony.

Never cut too early.

---

# Character Framing

Buddy

Centered

Leader of the frame.

Luna

Balanced and composed.

Ben

Slightly off-center while thinking.

Percy

Dynamic framing.

Occasional movement.

Felix

More negative space.

Executive presence.

---

# Rule of Thirds

Place characters near intersection points.

Avoid placing every subject in the center.

Leave headroom.

Maintain breathing space.

---

# Headroom

Keep comfortable space above the character.

Avoid cropping ears.

Avoid excessive empty space.

---

# Lead Room

Characters should have space in the direction they are looking.

Never place them against the edge of the frame.

---

# Focus

Keep speaking character in focus.

Background softly blurred when appropriate.

Maintain readable facial expressions.

---

# Depth

Scenes should include:

Foreground

Subject

Background

Avoid flat compositions.

---

# Office Composition

Include recognizable office elements:

Computers

Monitors

Plants

Coffee mugs

Whiteboards

Glass walls

Meeting tables

These reinforce the workplace setting.

---

# Scene Transitions

Preferred

Straight Cut

Match Cut

Fade (rare)

Avoid flashy transitions.

The storytelling should remain seamless.

---

# Camera Speed

All movements should be smooth.

Avoid:

Fast zooms

Violent shakes

Random spins

Unless the story specifically requires them.

---

# Character Eye Line

Characters should maintain believable eye contact.

Do not have characters constantly look into the camera.

Breaking the fourth wall should be intentional and rare.

---

# Studio Standard

Every camera movement must answer one question:

**"Does this help tell the story?"**

If the answer is no, remove the movement.

Simple, intentional cinematography creates stronger storytelling than complex camera work.

The audience should remember the characters and the joke—not the camera.
